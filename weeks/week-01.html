Day 1: What is Motion? Setting the Stage

50 minutes Introductory

🔑 What is Motion?

Motion is the change in position of an object with respect to time and relative to a reference frame.

💡

Key Insight: Motion is always relative! A baseball may be moving at 95 mph relative to home plate, but stationary relative to the pitcher's hand during the wind-up.

🎯 Key Topics

📍

Reference Frames

Understanding motion depends on your point of view - stadium vs. dugout vs. ball perspective

📐

Kinematics Introduction

Study of motion without considering what causes it - position, velocity, acceleration

⚾

Baseball Physics Setup

Key measurements: 60'6" pitcher's mound, 90' bases, typical pitch speeds 70-105 mph

🔄

Motion Types in Baseball

Linear (ball flight), rotational (bat swing), projectile (home run), oscillating (pitcher's rhythm)

🔑 Key Concept: Distance vs Displacement

Understanding these two concepts is fundamental to analyzing baseball motion:

📏

Distance

Scalar

Definition: Total path length traveled

✓ Always positive
✓ Adds up all movement
✓ No direction needed
1st → 2nd → 3rd
90 ft + 90 ft = 180 ft
VS
🎯

Displacement

Vector

Definition: Change in position with direction

✓ Can be positive or negative
✓ Straight-line measurement
✓ Includes direction
1st ↗ 3rd

Key Takeaway: Distance tells us how much ground was covered, while displacement tells us where you ended up relative to where you started.

🧮 Mathematical Tools & Formulas

📊 Kinematic Equations

Apply these fundamental equations to analyze baseball trajectories and predict ball positions.

v = u + at
Velocity-Time Relationship

Find final velocity when you know initial velocity, acceleration, and time

Use when: You need to find how fast a ball is traveling after a specific time period Baseball example: A pitch accelerates from 0 to 45 m/s in 0.15 seconds
s = ut + ½at²
Displacement Equation

Calculate distance traveled with constant acceleration

Use when: You need to find how far an object moves during acceleration Baseball example: How far does a ball travel during its acceleration phase in the pitcher's hand
v² = u² + 2as
Velocity-Displacement Relationship

Find final velocity without knowing time

Use when: Time is unknown but you know the distance and acceleration Baseball example: Find exit velocity when a ball travels 120 meters under gravity's deceleration
🔤 Variable Key:
v final velocity
u initial velocity
a acceleration
t time
s displacement
⚾ Example: Predicting where a fly ball will land

Given: A fly ball is hit with initial velocity u = 30 m/s upward, gravity a = -9.8 m/s²

Step 1: Find maximum height using v² = u² + 2as (at max height, v = 0)
0² = 30² + 2(-9.8)s → s = 900 ÷ 19.6 = 45.9 m high
Step 2: Find flight time using v = u + at
0 = 30 + (-9.8)t → t = 3.06 s up, total flight = 6.12 seconds

🧮 Calculus in Motion Physics

Calculus is the mathematical language of motion. Understanding derivatives and integrals unlocks the deep relationships between position, velocity, and acceleration.

📈

Derivatives: Finding Rates of Change

The Derivative Connection: Derivatives measure how one quantity changes with respect to another - exactly what we need for motion!

v = dx/dt Velocity is the derivative of position
⚾ Baseball Example: A runner's velocity tells us how quickly their position on the basepath is changing.
a = dv/dt = d²x/dt² Acceleration is the derivative of velocity
⚾ Baseball Example: A pitch accelerates as it leaves the pitcher's hand, then decelerates due to air resistance.
📊 Real Baseball Calculation:

Scenario: A fastball's position equation: x(t) = 15t + 2t²

Step 1 - Find Velocity: v(t) = dx/dt = 15 + 4t
Step 2 - Find Acceleration: a(t) = dv/dt = 4 m/s²
Result: The ball has constant acceleration of 4 m/s²
📊

Integrals: Finding Accumulated Change

The Integral Connection: Integrals sum up small changes over time - perfect for finding total displacement from velocity!

x = ∫ v dt Position is the integral of velocity
⚾ Baseball Example: To find how far a runner travels, we integrate their velocity over time.
v = ∫ a dt Velocity is the integral of acceleration
⚾ Baseball Example: A ball's final velocity depends on integrating acceleration over the acceleration period.
📊 Real Baseball Calculation:

Scenario: A home run's velocity: v(t) = 30 - 9.8t (accounting for gravity)

Step 1 - Setup Integral: x(t) = ∫(30 - 9.8t)dt
Step 2 - Integrate: x(t) = 30t - 4.9t² + C
Result: Position equation shows parabolic trajectory

🎯 The Big Picture: Calculus Chain of Motion

Position (x) meters
→ derivative d/dt
Velocity (v) m/s
→ derivative d/dt
Acceleration (a) m/s²
Acceleration (a) m/s²
← integral ∫ dt
Velocity (v) m/s
← integral ∫ dt
Position (x) meters

🚀 Advanced Applications in Baseball Physics

📈 Velocity-Time Graphs

Calculus Insight: The area under a v-t graph equals displacement (integral). The slope of a v-t graph equals acceleration (derivative).

Baseball Application: Analyzing a pitcher's arm velocity throughout the throwing motion.
📊 Optimization Problems

Calculus Insight: Finding maximum/minimum values using derivatives (setting derivative = 0).

Baseball Application: Finding the optimal launch angle for maximum home run distance.
🎯 Related Rates

Calculus Insight: How multiple variables change with respect to each other over time.

Baseball Application: How the distance between runner and base changes as they round the bases.
💡
Key Takeaway:

Calculus isn't just abstract math - it's the precise language that describes how baseball motion works. Every swing, pitch, and catch involves the fundamental relationship between position, velocity, and acceleration that calculus makes mathematically exact.

🎭 Types of Motion in Baseball

Understanding these four fundamental motion types will help you analyze any baseball play.

➡️

Linear Motion

Definition: Motion in a straight line from one point to another

Key Feature: Constant direction of movement

Baseball Examples:
  • 🎯 Fastball from pitcher to catcher
  • 🏃‍♂️ Runner sprinting to first base
  • ⚾ Line drive hit to outfield
🔄

Rotational Motion

Definition: Motion around a fixed axis or center point

Key Feature: Circular or spinning movement

Baseball Examples:
  • 🤾‍♂️ Pitcher's arm during windup
  • ⚾ Ball spinning after release
  • 🏏 Batter's swing motion
📈

Projectile Motion

Definition: Curved motion under gravity's influence

Key Feature: Parabolic trajectory path

Baseball Examples:
  • 🏟️ Home run ball flight
  • 🎈 Pop fly to infield
  • 🎯 Outfielder's throw to home
🌀

Combined Motion

Definition: Multiple motion types happening simultaneously

Key Feature: Complex movement patterns

Baseball Examples:
  • ⚾ Curveball (projectile + spin)
  • 🏏 Slider pitch movement
  • 🎯 Knuckleball trajectory

🔍 Analysis Tip:

Most baseball actions involve combined motion. For example, a curveball has both projectile motion (gravity acting on the ball) and rotational motion (spin affecting the ball's path).

🔄 Converting m/s to mph
1

Start with: 46.10 m/s

2

Use conversion factor: 1 m/s = 2.237 mph

How we get 2.237:

• 1 meter = 3.28084 feet

• 1 hour = 3600 seconds

• 1 mile = 5,280 feet

• So: 1 m/s = (3.28084 ft/s) × (3600 s/hr) ÷ (5280 ft/mile)

• Calculate: 3.28084 × 3600 ÷ 5280 = 2.237 mph

3

Multiply: 46.10 × 2.237 = 103.1 mph

🎯

Final Answer: The fastball travels at 46.10 m/s or 103.1 mph

💡

Quick tip: To convert m/s to mph, multiply by 2.237 (or approximately 2.24)
